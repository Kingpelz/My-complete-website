Here are the list of 10 best Nigerian food

  • Jollof Rice


    • Jollof Rice is Nigeria’s favorite local dish! This iconic meal, known for its rich tomato base and aromatic spices, is loved across the country. It’s a staple at parties, weddings, and everyday meals. With 21% of Nigerians selecting it as their top dish, Jollof Rice remains a symbol of West African pride and it is the number one in the top five Nigerian foods

  • Egusi Soup


    • Egusi Soup is a thick, hearty dish made from ground melon seeds, vegetables, and spices. It pairs well with pounded yam or eba, making it one of the favorite food examples in Nigerian households. 14% of Nigerians favor this delicious soup.

  • Iyan (Pounded Yam)


    • A smooth, stretchy dough-like dish made by pounding boiled yams, Iyan is often enjoyed with various Nigerian soups like egusi and ogbono. It shares second place with Egusi Soup, receiving 14% of votes in our survey.

  • Amala


    • Popular in the southwestern region, Amala is made from yam or plantain flour and is known for its slightly bitter taste. It is best enjoyed with ewedu soup, making it a top choice for 9% of Nigerians.

  • Banga Soup


    • Banga Soup, native to the Niger Delta region, is made from palm nut extract and flavored with local spices. This richly aromatic dish is loved by 8% of Nigerians.

  • Abacha


    • Also known as African Salad, Abacha is a cassava-based dish widely consumed in the southeastern parts of Nigeria. This refreshing and nutritious dish was selected by 6% of Nigerians as their favorite meal.

  • Moi Moi


    • Moi Moi is a steamed bean pudding made from blended black-eyed peas, peppers, and spices. It is high in protein and often served with Jollof Rice or Garri. Another 6% of Nigerians favor this delicious meal.

  • Eba


    • Eba is a staple made from cassava flour and is commonly paired with soups like Egusi, Okra, and Ogbono. Despite its simplicity, it remains a favorite for 4% of Nigerians.

  • Fufu


    • Fufu is another starchy staple made from fermented cassava. Its smooth and elastic texture makes it a popular accompaniment for Nigerian soups. It ties with Eba at 4%.

  • Ekuru


    • Finally, Ekuru, also called white Moi Moi, is similar to Moi Moi but without palm oil, giving it a distinct flavor. It is a traditional Yoruba dish loved by 3% of Nigerians.
